About this register

A companion to The Guardian's reporting on derelict churches.

"Abandoned" is not a category that appears in any of the official records. A closed church might be redundant to one body, at risk on another's register, and in the same year vested in a conservation charity that will spend a decade on its roof. This register tries to hold those categories together on one map so that a reader can see what is actually happening to a building, not only what happened to its paperwork.

What the status colours mean

  • At riskThe building has serious condition problems and no funded plan to fix them. Most often from Historic England's Heritage at Risk register, or equivalent.
  • RescuedRecently taken into conservation care — a new roof or vesting agreement within the last few years.
  • PreservedHeld long-term by a conservation trust. No longer in regular worship, but the fabric is being actively maintained.
  • RepurposedThe building is in active use, but not as a church. A mosque, a community centre, flats.
  • Closed, unresolvedNo longer in use; no plan yet for what happens next. The holding pattern that precedes either rescue or loss.
  • LostDemolished, or so far gone that the building no longer exists in any meaningful sense. Included for memory.

Sources the register draws from

  • National Churches Trust — the closest thing to an all-denomination census, with condition survey data.
  • Historic England Heritage at Risk — the statutory at-risk register for England.
  • Friends of Friendless Churches — the charity that rescues and holds ~70 churches in England and Wales.
  • Churches Conservation Trust — ~350 redundant CofE churches in permanent care.
  • Church in Wales — the redundant churches list, including St Tyfrydog's.
  • Buildings at Risk Register for Scotland — the Scottish equivalent, run by the Scottish Civic Trust.
  • DataMapWales / Cadw — Welsh historic assets.
  • Planning Data API — England-wide listed buildings and heritage layers joined on UPRN.

How to contribute

Every building page has an Add to the record button. It sends your note to an editorial queue — nothing is published automatically. We are particularly interested in:

  • Photographs of churches from your area, especially ones that aren't widely documented.
  • Memories of services, weddings, funerals, harvest festivals — the ordinary life of a building.
  • Corrections. If a building on the register has changed status, or is already in a better state than we describe, tell us.
  • Tip-offs. A closed church you're worried about. A chapel that was quietly demolished. A rescue we haven't heard of.

Editorial notes on the prototype

This version is a seed. Six buildings, hand-researched from public sources and the Guardian's reporting. As the series grows and the data pipelines connect — to the Heritage at Risk API, to the National Churches Trust's Church Heritage Record, to the conservation charities — the register will widen and the per-building detail will deepen. Until then, anywhere a field reads provenance, that's the honest record of where the information came from and how far we would stand behind it.

The basemap is OpenStreetMap, styled down toward the newsprint palette. Coordinates for each building are to village or street level; the map marker is not a surveyor's pin.
